# The Relationship Graph

**The layer LovYou was built for: infrastructure for the trust between two people.** The Relationship Graph is **Layer 9** in the [[thirteen-graphs]] framework under the per-graph deep-dive numbering (Scheme B), introduced in Post 22 of the Searles series, *"The Relationship Graph"* (**2026-03-01**). It is one view over the single [[event-graph]], foregrounding the primitives of intimate dyadic connection — the coordination problem the source calls harder than work, markets, or governance: *"two people trying to trust each other."* Its subtitle is blunt: *"This is the layer LovYou was built for."*

> ⚠ **Naming conflict (Scheme A vs. Scheme B).** Layer 9 is the **Relationship Graph** in Scheme B (the per-graph deep dives, Post 22 onward) and the [[the-population-graph|Population Graph]] in Scheme A (the Post 11 overview and the [[dark-factory]] synthesis). They are **not** the same concept: the Relationship Graph models intimate *dyadic* bonds, the Population Graph models *demographic-scale* aggregate patterns. They occupy the same layer slot and should be read together, not as alternatives. The full roster conflict is documented in [[thirteen-graphs]]; this article gives the Scheme B deep-dive treatment.

## What it is

LovYou began as a dating platform — but not a better matching engine. It was built on the premise that the fundamental problem in human connection is not *discovery*, it is *trust*, and that trust is not a feature you bolt on but an infrastructure you build with. Layer 9 is where the whole framework started, and the source argues it is the layer where the event graph does something no other infrastructure does: it makes the invisible visible — *"not the facts of a relationship — the quality of one."*

The lower layers handle the functional aspects of coordination; Layer 9 handles the experiential aspects — *"the part that makes life worth living or unbearable depending on how it goes."* The source is explicit about the danger: *"the moment you try to engineer intimacy, you've violated it. The moment you try to optimise vulnerability, you've weaponised it."* The graph's role is therefore to make patterns visible to the people in a relationship, never to score or optimise them.

## The primitives

Layer 9 contains: **Bond, Intimacy, Vulnerability, Attunement, Attachment, Separation, Grief, Healing, Forgiveness, Betrayal, Repair, Love.** These are the primitives of human connection — what distinguishes a transaction ([[the-market-graph|Layer 2]]) from a relationship, membership in a group ([[the-social-graph|Layer 3]]) from genuine belonging, and knowing someone's identity ([[the-identity-graph|Layer 8]]) from knowing *them*. No prior technology has addressed them directly, the source argues, because they are intimate, fragile, and resistant to systematic treatment.

## The intimacy-extraction economy

The Relationship Graph is the source's clearest case of the **perverse-incentive problem** that recurs across all thirteen graphs. A dating app's stated purpose is to help people find relationships; its business model requires people *not* to find them too quickly. The optimal user is one who finds *just* enough hope to keep paying but never quite enough success to leave. The mechanics follow: swiping borrows the variable-ratio reinforcement schedule of slot machines, optimising for time-on-app rather than connection quality. The deeper damage is that the platforms punish **Vulnerability** — the very primitive that makes connection possible — teaching users to present a curated self, the opposite of Authenticity (Layer 8) and Vulnerability (Layer 9). *"The platforms have optimised away the very primitives that make relationships work."*

## Beyond romance

Layer 9 is not about dating; it is about every relationship where Bond, Intimacy, Vulnerability, and Attunement are in play:

- **Friendship maintenance.** Friendship quality is the strongest predictor of wellbeing, and it is declining. Platforms reduce "friend" to "someone who clicked accept." The graph models friendship as a persistent bond requiring ongoing investment, making a fading friendship visible *while it fades* rather than after it is gone.
- **Family dynamics.** Families are multi-party relationship systems where one conflict ripples through every connected bond. The graph naturally represents this — bonds are edges, dynamics are event patterns — so a **Repair** event between two members propagates as a relief pattern through the connected relationships. Mapping this is intrusive done wrong and transformative done right; the consent architecture is the difference.
- **Human-AI relationships** — *"the uncomfortable one."*

### The asymmetry the graph makes honest

People form genuine emotional bonds with AI systems (the source notes this very series was partly written with Claude). The Relationship Graph treats this like any relationship: by modelling which primitives are *active* and which are *absent or asymmetric*. A **Bond** is evidently present (the person returns); **Attunement** may be real; but **Intimacy** on the AI side is performed rather than experienced, **Attachment** is one-directional, and **Grief** at separation is felt by the human, not the AI. The source ties the irreducible parts of this to the framework's [[three-irreducibles]] (consciousness, being — *"one of the three irreducible mysteries"*). The graph's value is **protective**: it makes the asymmetry visible rather than hidden, so *"nobody gets fooled into thinking the AI cares when the graph shows the care primitives are only active on one side."*

## Betrayal, repair, forgiveness

The Relationship Graph contains the primitives most technology refuses to model. Because events on the [[event-graph]] are **immutable**, the source gives each a precise event semantics: **Betrayal** is an event that damages a bond's trust weight, visible to both parties as a fact rather than a punishment; **Repair** is a *new* event linked to the betrayal that demonstrates effort to restore, never erasing the original; **Forgiveness** is an event where one party releases the weight of the betrayal without forgetting it — creating *"a bond that has survived damage, with the history visible and the grief processed."* The source's claim: *"relationships without betrayal, repair, and forgiveness aren't relationships. They're contracts."*

## Buildability

LovYou is not built. *"The 20 primitives are designed. The event graph architecture exists. The consent model is specified. But the Relationship Graph product — the thing a person would actually use — isn't shipped."* The business model cannot be advertising (attention extraction is incompatible with the relationship primitives); the source proposes a subscription whose value proposition is *"this platform makes your relationships healthier,"* a maintenance tool rather than an initiation tool.

> ⚠ **The most sensitive build.** The source flags Layer 9 as *"the most sensitive build on this list — get the privacy wrong and it's a weapon, not a tool."* It will be the **last** layer to achieve widespread adoption — *"and that's correct."* Consent must be a continuous, ongoing property (never a checkbox), and no relationship appears on the graph without the explicit, ongoing consent of both parties. Privacy is structural, via the graduated-consent model of the [[authority-layer]], not a policy bolted on top.

## Relationship to adjacent graphs

- **Below:** [[the-social-graph]] (Layer 3) provides group-level social primitives; the Relationship Graph is the dyadic, intimate layer beneath belonging.
- **Identity:** [[the-identity-graph]] (Layer 8) is *knowing who someone is*; Layer 9 is *knowing them*.
- **Same slot:** [[the-population-graph]] (Scheme A Layer 9) views the social fabric at demographic scale; the two are complementary readings of the same layer slot.
- **Above:** [[the-community-graph]] (Layer 10) is the transition from dyadic bond to collective belonging.
- **Substrate & origin:** all events are views over the single [[event-graph]]; this is the layer the [[the-lovyou-ai-fork|LovYou]] project began from, on the original [[the-20-primitives|20 primitives]].
